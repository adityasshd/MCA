## **Study Guide: Unit 02 - Average**

## **1. Objective**
After studying this unit, learners should be able to:

- Understand how to compute the **average** of a set of numbers.
- Learn about **weighted averages**, which take into account different weights assigned to values.
- Apply formulas and shortcuts for computing averages efficiently.
- Solve problems involving changes in averages due to replacement, removal, or addition of elements.

## **2. Introduction**
An **average** is a measure used to represent a large amount of data using a single number. It is commonly used in real-life scenarios such as calculating average scores, temperatures, ages, etc.

Key examples of average usage:
- **Marks in exams**: Calculated as total marks divided by the number of subjects.
- **Age of a class**: Calculated as the sum of ages of all students divided by the number of students.
- **Temperature over a period**: Average temperature is calculated by dividing total temperature by the number of days.

## **3. Core Concepts**

## **3.1. Average**
The formula for the average is:
$$ \text{Average} = \frac{\text{Sum of Quantities}}{\text{Number of Quantities}} $$

## **Examples:**
- **First 40 Natural Numbers**:
    $$
    \text{Sum} = \frac{n(n+1)}{2},\quad \text{where } n = 40
    $$
    $$
    \text{Average} = \frac{40 \times 41}{2 \times 40} = 20.5
    $$

- **Prime Numbers Between 30 and 50**:
    Prime numbers: 31, 37, 41, 43, 47
    $$
    \text{Average} = \frac{31 + 37 + 41 + 43 + 47}{5} = \frac{199}{5} = 39.8
    $$

## **3.2. Weighted Average**
When different items have different "weights" (importance), the weighted average is computed as:
$$
\text{Weighted Average} = \frac{\sum (\text{Value} \times \text{Weight})}{\sum (\text{Weights})}
$$

## **Example:**
A man bought:
- 5 toys @ ₹200 each → Total = ₹1000
- 6 toys @ ₹250 each → Total = ₹1500
- 9 toys @ ₹300 each → Total = ₹2700

$$
\text{Total Cost} = 1000 + 1500 + 2700 = ₹5200,\quad \text{Total Toys} = 20
$$
$$
\text{Average Cost per Toy} = \frac{5200}{20} = ₹260
$$

## **4. Shortcuts and Formulas**

| Formula | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| $\text{Sum of Quantities} = \text{Average} \times \text{Number of Quantities}$ | Used to calculate sum from average and count |
| $\text{Number of Quantities} = \frac{\text{Sum of Quantities}}{\text{Average}}$ | Used to calculate count from sum and average |
| $\text{Combined Average} = \frac{n_1x + n_2y}{n_1 + n_2}$ | For combining two groups |
| $\text{New Average} = x + a$, where $x$ is original average and $a$ is constant change | Applies to linear transformations |
| $\text{Replacement Rule}: q = p + n(y - x)$ | When replacing an item with another |

## **5. Special Cases and Rules**

| Case | Formula | Description |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Average of First n Natural Numbers** | $\frac{n+1}{2}$ | Simple arithmetic progression |
| **Average of Squares of First n Natural Numbers** | $\frac{(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}$ | Based on known summation formula |
| **Average of Cubes of First n Natural Numbers** | $\frac{n(n+1)^2}{4}$ | Another known summation formula |
| **Average of Odd Numbers up to n** | $\frac{\text{Last Odd Number} + 1}{2}$ | Direct computation |
| **Average of Even Numbers up to n** | $\frac{\text{Last Even Number} + 2}{2}$ | Direct computation |
| **Average of Consecutive Numbers** | Middle number (if n is odd), average of middle two (if n is even) | Special case rule |

## **6. Examples and Applications**

## **Example 1: Combined Average**
Two classes:
- Class A: 36 students, average weight = 40 kg
- Class B: 44 students, average weight = 35 kg

$$
\text{Combined Average} = \frac{36 \times 40 + 44 \times 35}{36 + 44}
= \frac{1440 + 1540}{80} = \frac{2980}{80} = 37.25 \, \text{kg}
$$

## **Example 2: Replacement Problem**
Average weight of 10 oarsmen = 53 kg
One member is replaced, increasing average by 1.8 kg

$$
\text{Increase in Weight} = 1.8 \times 10 = 18 \, \text{kg}
\Rightarrow \text{New Man's Weight} = 53 + 18 = 71 \, \text{kg}
$$

## **Example 3: Age of Teacher**
Average age of 24 students and a teacher = 16 years
Excluding the teacher, average drops by 1 year

$$
\text{Teacher's Age} = 25(16 - 15) + 15 = 25 + 15 = 40 \, \text{years}
$$
